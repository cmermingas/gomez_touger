Florida case on drug charges goes to Supreme Court

The Fourth Amendment provides the protection against unreasonable searches and seizures. The Supreme Court recently heard a case on the use of a drug-sniffing dog on a residential porch without the permission of the owners. The case questioned the scope of these protections and, ultimately, the justices held that this type of a search was unconstitutional.

Any evidence found from an unconstitutional search is dismissed. In some cases, this can also lead to the dismissal of criminal charges.

Details of the case

The case, Florida v. Joelis Jardines, began with an unverified tip that marijuana was being grown in the home of Joelis Jardines. A month after receiving the tip, a detective who was also a trained canine handler brought a drug sniffing dog to the residence to conduct a free air sniff search. The dog alerted to the presence of illegal substances at the home's front door.

Based on this alert, the detective was able to obtain a warrant to search the home. The search led to finding multiple marijuana plants and drug trafficking charges for Jardines.

When reviewing this case, the court had to determine if a search occurred. The court defines a search as a physical intrusion by the government on "persons, houses, papers or effects." The state argued that the front porch, where the free air sniff was conducted, did not qualify within the definition of a home. If the porch was not part of the home, it would not receive these protections.

The Supreme Court disagreed. Instead, the justices defined the porch as part of a home's curtilage. Curtilage is a legal term referring to the "area immediately surrounding and associated with the home." Areas that fit this definition receive protection under the Fourth Amendment. As a result, the enforcement officer's use of a narcotics dog on the porch qualifies as a search. Since a search was conducted, the Fourth Amendment applied. When the justices applied this law, they found the police violated Jardines' right to privacy by conducting the search.

Jardines' impact on drug charges in Florida

When police are found in violation of the law, anything found during the search is tainted. As a result, evidence found during the search cannot be admitted. In cases similar to Jardines', evidence like the marijuana plants would not be allowed. This could result in dropping the drug trafficking charges.

Those charged with cannabis drug trafficking crimes in Florida face serious penalties. Penalties include a felony conviction and:

•· Minimum three years imprisonment and $25,000 fine for 25 pounds or more

•· Minimum seven years imprisonment and $50,000 fine for 2,000 pounds

•· Minimum 15 years in prison and $200,000 fine for 10,000 pounds

A strong defense to criminal charges can lead to a reduction or even dismissal of the charges. If you are charged with a crime in Florida, contact an experienced Florida criminal defense lawyer to better ensure a strong defense to the charges.
